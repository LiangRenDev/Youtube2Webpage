TESLA AI · ASHOK ELLUSWAMY · ICCV 2025

A Peek into Tesla's Autonomous Future: Core Tech Revealed by VP Ashok Elluswamy

SUMMARY

Tesla VP Ashok Elluswamy reveals the full end-to-end neural network stack powering production vehicles today—from raw camera pixels to steering output—plus the world simulator used to train and evaluate it. Key topics: fleet data at scale (“the Niagara Falls of data”), 3D Gaussian splatting for scene reconstruction, closed-loop reinforcement learning, and transfer of the same architecture to Tesla's Optimus humanoid robot. Milestones covered: commercial robotaxi launch in Austin and the SF Bay Area, and production vehicles autonomously driving off the assembly line.

INTRO & MILESTONES

Tesla VP Ashok Elluswamy opened by recounting a landmark year for the company's autonomous driving program, beginning with the commercial launch of a fully driverless robotaxi service in Austin and the San Francisco Bay Area — a real-world deployment where passengers can hail a Tesla with no human in the driver's seat. The team also demonstrated end-to-end vehicle delivery, autonomously piloting a newly manufactured Tesla from the factory floor across highways, city streets, and parking lots to a customer's front door using only production cameras and the same onboard computer found in every retail car. Elluswamy emphasized that these are not research prototypes but production vehicles shipping today, underlining the team's belief that the current neural-network-based architecture is mature enough for widespread deployment.

END-TO-END ARCHITECTURE

At the core of Tesla's self-driving system is a fully end-to-end neural network: raw camera streams from eight surrounding cameras flow directly through the network, which outputs steering, braking, and acceleration commands without any intermediate hand-crafted representation. Elluswamy explained why Tesla abandoned its earlier modular, explicit-modeling approach — codifying human driving preferences in code proved intractable, since the right behavior depends on context in ways that resist simple rules. The end-to-end architecture solves this by learning alignment with human values directly from data, and it also eliminates the lossy interface between traditional perception and planning modules, allowing uncertainty to propagate seamlessly through the whole system. A further engineering benefit is hardware homogeneity: a single class of compute handles the entire pipeline, simplifying deployment and scaling across the global fleet.

DATA AT SCALE

Tesla's central competitive advantage in data collection is its massive fleet of production vehicles, which continuously streams real-world driving scenarios at a scale Elluswamy described as rivaling Niagara Falls in throughput. Rather than simply reacting to hazards, the trained model demonstrates second-order predictive reasoning — in one striking example, the system began braking for a car that had not yet lost control, detecting the precursor signs of a spin-out before the vehicle struck the barrier, a judgment call that required modeling the downstream consequences of an unstable trajectory. This kind of proactive, anticipatory safety behavior emerges naturally from training on diverse fleet data and cannot easily be programmed explicitly, illustrating why scale and data diversity are treated as first-class engineering priorities.

WORLD SIMULATOR

To overcome the scarcity of rare and dangerous training scenarios, Tesla developed a learned world simulator: a single neural network that, given a past sequence of driving states and a set of control actions, synthesizes photorealistic video from all eight surrounding cameras simultaneously and in a geometrically consistent manner. The simulator is trained on easy-to-collect state-action pairs — the quality of the driving footage is irrelevant, since the goal is to generate plausible future states for any given action sequence. By connecting the simulator to the policy network in a closed loop, Tesla can let the car 'drive' through entirely synthetic environments for extended periods, generating dense reinforcement-learning signal without the cost and risk of on-road collection. Elluswamy highlighted the system's ability to produce consistent, steerable six-minute video rollouts, unlocking both scalable evaluation and RL fine-tuning.

ROBOTS & OPTIMUS

Looking ahead, Elluswamy outlined Tesla's ambition to unlock the full fleet of consumer vehicles for unsupervised autonomy, alongside the upcoming Cyber Cab — a two-seat robotaxi purpose-built for the lowest per-mile transportation cost, powered by the same scalable neural-network stack. He then broadened the scope to humanoid robotics: the same end-to-end architecture that drives Tesla's cars transfers with minimal modification to the Optimus robot, whose manipulation and locomotion policies share underlying representations with the automotive system. This unified approach to embodied intelligence — one architecture spanning road vehicles and bipedal robots — is Elluswamy's thesis for why the scale and investment in self-driving pays dividends well beyond transportation.
